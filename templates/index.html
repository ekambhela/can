🚧 Work in progress

This is an evolving research project

Karkive is under active development and subject to many changes. The model, the underlying data, the drug panel, and the design are all moving targets, expect it to look and behave differently as it grows. The drug panel now spans the full GDSC screen — 248 distinct compounds across 24 target pathways (replicate screens merged). Feedback and rough edges are part of the process.

The vision

Why I built Karkive

01

No two tumors are really the same

I keep coming back to one idea: two people can get the exact same diagnosis and still have cancers running on completely different wiring. So it never sat right with me that treatment so often follows the average patient instead of the tumor in front of you. I wanted to build toward the version where it follows the biology.

02

We're not there yet, honestly

And it's worth being upfront that we're not there yet. A lot of treatment still starts from a standard playbook. The targeted matching we do have leans on a handful of biomarkers, the testing isn't evenly available, and plenty of care still comes down to trial and error. The promise is real; the day-to-day is still slow, patchy, and imprecise.

03

Where Karkive comes in

Karkive is my small attempt at chipping away at that gap. The idea is simple: take a tumor's genomic profile, learn from real drug-response data, and suggest which drugs it's most likely to respond to, ranked, explained, and upfront about what it doesn't know. It's early and imperfect, but I'd rather build it in the open and keep improving than wait around for perfect. One sample in, the right treatment out.

Why match at all?

Three reasons the molecular profile, not the organ, should steer treatment.

Cancer isn't one disease

A breast tumor driven by HER2 behaves nothing like one driven by BRCA loss. The driver mutation often decides what works far more than where the tumor sits.

Trial-and-error is expensive

Every cycle on an ineffective regimen is time a tumor keeps growing. Matching therapy to biology up front is the entire promise of precision oncology.

Black boxes don't belong in medicine

Every Karkive recommendation ships with the biomarkers that drove it and an honest uncertainty band. No mystery scores; you can see the reasoning.

Methodology

How Karkive thinks

A transparent pipeline built on a well-worn idea in pharmacogenomics: predict how a tumor responds to every drug, then rank.

1 · Profile the tumor

A sample is described by the features GDSC records for every cell line: driver-gene mutations (TP53, KRAS, EGFR, BRAF, ALK, ERBB2, BRCA1/2, PIK3CA, PTEN, NRAS, APC, CDKN2A), ERBB2/HER2 amplification, MSI status, and the tissue of origin. Anything you leave out is treated as not-present and flagged.

2 · Predict drug response

Karkive frames this as drug-response prediction: for each of the 248 drugs, a gradient-boosted model is trained on the real cell lines that were screened against it, predicting how sensitive a profile is (from the drug's measured IC50 values). Modeling each drug on its own screened lines handles the fact that not every drug was tested on every line.

3 · Rank with calibrated uncertainty

Drugs are ranked by predicted sensitivity, shown as a percentile ("more sensitive than X% of cell lines"), with an uncertainty band from the model's held-out residuals. A decision margin tells you how clearly the top pick beats the runner-up. A wide margin is a confident call; a narrow one says “these are close.”

4 · Explain every call

For the recommended therapy, Karkive reports a signed, quantified attribution: how many percentage points each biomarker added or subtracted, split into supporting factors and resistance cautions. The recommendation is a glass box, not a black one.

Three ways to ask

Upload a file

Drop a single tumor as CSV, TSV, or JSON. Wide, key-value, or one-record formats all parse.

Fill in a form

No file? Pick a tissue and toggle the mutations and markers; the form is generated from the model's own schema.

Score a cohort

Upload one tumor per row and get a ranked recommendation table for the whole group, exportable to CSV.

An honest footnote. Karkive learns from real GDSC data, but from cancer cell lines, not patients, and from a limited biomarker panel. It recovers real biology (see the science page) yet its accuracy is modest, exactly what you'd expect when predicting drug response from a handful of markers. It demonstrates the workflow; it is not a clinical predictor.

The science behind the match

The therapy panel, the biomarker rules that ground the model, and how well it actually performs.

The therapy panel

All 248 distinct GDSC compounds, spanning 24 target pathways — from broad cytotoxics to precisely targeted kinase inhibitors.

The GDSC screen covers 265 compounds; we name 264 from the public GDSC screened-compounds annotation and, after merging the 16 compounds screened twice into one denoised target each, model 248 distinct drugs — so every ranked drug carries an authoritative name and target pathway.

Signal the model learns from the real data

These associations are measured directly in the GDSC cell lines (mutant vs wild-type IC50), not hand-written.

BRAF mutation→BRAF inhibitors (Dabrafenib, PLX-4720) · p<10⁻⁶
NRAS mutation→MEK/BRAF-inhibitor sensitivity · p<10⁻⁶
ERBB2 amplification→HER2 inhibitors (Afatinib, CP724714)
TP53 mutation→shifts Paclitaxel response
Tissue of origin→strong baseline signal
EGFR mutation⊣weak in cell lines (few EGFR⁺)

            The origin story
            
        Why I'm building Karkive
        
            
        
              Cancer isn't abstract to me. It has reached into my own family and taken people I love,
              and I've watched up close how much of treatment can still feel like a search in the dark —
              trying one thing, waiting, then trying the next. That experience is the reason this project
              exists.
            
        
            
        
              I'm a biology freshman at San Diego State, and I'm not going to pretend I have this all
              figured out. But I'd rather start building toward the future I want to see — where a
              tumor's own biology, not the average patient, decides the treatment — than wait until
              I'm "qualified enough." Karkive is me teaching myself the science and the machine learning
              in public, one version at a time.
            
        
            
        
              It's early, it's imperfect, and every rough edge here is a thing I'm still learning. But
              the goal behind it is completely serious: to chip away, however slightly, at the gap
              between how cancer is treated today and how precisely it could be.
            
        
          
        
        
        
      
        

      
        
        The dream is simple: a world where beating cancer is a matter of reading the
        biology correctly, not guessing. Karkive is my first swing at it.
